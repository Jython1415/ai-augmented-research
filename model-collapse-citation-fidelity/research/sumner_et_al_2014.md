# Sumner et al. (2014) BMJ: Exaggeration in Health-Related Science News and Academic Press Releases

## Full Citation
Sumner P, Vivian-Griffiths S, Boivin J, et al. **The association between exaggeration in health related science news and academic press releases: retrospective observational study.** *BMJ*. 2014;349:g7015. https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.g7015

PubMed ID: 25498121
PMC ID: PMC4262123

## Study Overview

This was a retrospective quantitative content analysis examining the source of exaggeration in health-related science reporting.

### Sample
- **462 press releases** from biomedical and health-related science issued by 20 leading UK universities in 2011
- **668 associated news stories**
- **Original journal articles** (peer-reviewed source material)

---

## Coding Categories: EXACT Specifications

The study operationalized three primary types of exaggeration, each with specific scaling systems:

### 1. **Advice to Readers** (Four-Level Scale)

The scale measured explicitness of advice to the general public:

1. **No advice** - No recommendation to readers or general public
2. **Implicit advice** - Advice that is suggested but not directly stated
3. **Explicit advice to non-public audiences** - Clear advice to healthcare professionals, researchers, or specialists
4. **Explicit advice to readers/general public** - Direct, clear advice to lay audience or general public

**Example categorization**: A statement "eating blueberries may improve memory" would be coded lower (implicit advice) than "you should eat blueberries daily to improve your memory" (explicit advice to public).

### 2. **Causal Claims from Correlational Data** (Seven-Point Determinism Scale)

The scale measured increasing levels of causal strength from the original correlational finding:

1. **No statement** - Relationship not mentioned
2. **Explicit non-relation** - Explicitly states no causal relationship exists
3. **Correlational** - Describes an association or correlation only
4. **Ambiguous** - Unclear whether causal or correlational
5. **Conditional causal** - "Can cause" or "may cause" language; hedged causality
6. **Can cause** - Direct statement of causal potential
7. **Unconditionally causal** - Strong causal language: "causes," "causes disease," without qualification

**Key distinction**: The scale captures how press releases and news stories amplify causality beyond what the original correlational study allows. A study showing "people who exercise have lower heart disease rates" (correlational) becomes exaggerated when reported as "exercise prevents heart disease" (unconditionally causal).

### 3. **Inference to Humans from Non-Human Studies** (Three-Level Scale)

For studies conducted on animals or non-human systems, the scale measured human applicability claims:

1. **Explicitly non-human** - Study clearly presented as animal/non-human research with no human claims
2. **Implicitly human** - Human applicability suggested without explicit statement (e.g., "a pregnant mother's stress..." in reference to animal study)
3. **Explicitly human** - Direct claim of applicability to humans

**Key distinction**: An animal study of stress in pregnancy might be reported as "proves stress harms fetal development" (explicit human inference) rather than "animal models suggest..." (explicitly non-human).

## Operationalization: Specific Coding Rules

### Data Extraction Protocol
- **Press releases and news**: Coded using **title and first two sentences** (inverted pyramid structure)
- **Journal articles**: Coded using **abstract and discussion sections**

This decision was made to capture the most impactful language that readers would encounter.

### Coding Effort
- **Each coding set took 3-4 hours** to complete (intensive per-study analysis)
- Coders evaluated:
  - Sample size
  - Study duration
  - Study completion/response rate
  - Source of quotes
  - Presence and nature of claims in each exaggeration category

### Training and Coders
The study does not explicitly state:
- The exact number of coders (though references to "research assistants" and principal investigators suggest a team)
- Formal training protocols detailed in the main paper

However, the extensive methodology (3-4 hours per coding set) suggests rigorous, careful training was necessary.

## Intercoder Reliability and Agreement

### Overall Reliability
- **Concordance rate: 91%** across double-coded materials
- **Mean Cohen's κ = 0.88** (indicating strong agreement)

### Double-Coding Protocol
- **27% of press releases and journal articles** were double-coded
- **21% of news stories** were double-coded

### Robustness Testing
The authors conducted simulations demonstrating that even if disagreement rates reached 10%, this would not substantively alter their main conclusions. This shows their agreement rates were not marginal to their findings.

### Per-Category Breakdown
The available documentation does not provide category-specific κ values, but the overall κ=0.88 suggests strong reliability across all three exaggeration types.

## Key Findings on Exaggeration Prevalence

These rates demonstrate the magnitude of the exaggeration problem:

### Exaggeration Rates in Press Releases
- **40% (95% CI: 33-46%)** contained exaggerated advice
- **33% (95% CI: 26-40%)** contained exaggerated causal claims
- **36% (95% CI: 28-46%)** contained exaggerated human inference from animal studies

### Downstream Effects in News
When exaggeration was present in press releases, news coverage amplified it:
- **58%** of news stories contained exaggerated advice (vs. 17% when PR had none)
- **81%** of news stories contained exaggerated causal claims (vs. 18% when PR had none)
- **86%** of news stories contained exaggerated human inference (vs. 10% when PR had none)

### Critical Implication
The 81-86% amplification rates for causal claims and human inference show that **exaggeration in press releases is highly infectious** in downstream news coverage.

## Mapping to Model Collapse Use Case: Social Media Posts Overgeneralizing Findings

### Key Parallels

1. **Causal exaggeration (7-point scale)** maps directly to social media posts that:
   - Take a correlational finding (e.g., "linked to higher rates of X")
   - Present it as definitive causation (e.g., "causes X")
   - Remove conditional language (e.g., "may increase risk" → "increases risk")

2. **Advice overgeneralization** maps to posts that:
   - Extract behavioral advice from observational studies
   - Omit population specificity (e.g., advice for pregnant women applied to all)
   - Present research recommendations as personal imperatives

3. **Human inference from limited contexts** maps to posts that:
   - Take findings from specific populations and generalize to all
   - Convert conditional/contextual findings into universal claims
   - Apply lab or animal-derived insights to human populations

### Methodological Strengths for Your Codebook

**The Sumner team's approach is exemplary because:**

1. **Multi-level scale design** (3, 4, and 7 points) captures nuance rather than binary classification
2. **Specific text locations** (title/first sentences) focus on high-impact language where overgeneralization occurs
3. **Strong reliability** (κ=0.88) demonstrates that careful operationalization produces replicable decisions
4. **Transparent materials** enable new projects to adopt similar scales or adapt them

### Potential Adaptations for Social Media Coding

- **Conditional language removal**: Code whether original conditional framing ("may," "associated with") is retained or stripped
- **Population specificity**: Track whether sample population characteristics are preserved or generalized
- **Mechanistic plausibility**: Distinguish posts that overstate mechanistic understanding beyond what the paper establishes
- **Aggregation across papers**: Code when a single post conflates findings across multiple studies with different populations/conditions

## Boundary Cases and Decision Rules

While the main paper does not extensively detail specific boundary cases, the 3-4 hour per-study coding time and κ=0.88 agreement suggest the coding manual included:

### Likely Categories of Boundary Cases

1. **Ambiguously causal language** - When passive voice or indirect construction makes causal intent unclear ("Benefits from X observed" vs. "X causes benefits")

2. **Hedged vs. Conditional** - Distinguishing between "may cause" (conditional causal, point 5) and "can cause" (point 6)

3. **Implicit vs. Explicit human inference** - Whether phrases like "this research suggests..." followed by human application constitute "implicit" or "explicit" inference

4. **Audience ambiguity** - When advice could plausibly target healthcare providers or lay public (implicit vs. explicit to public)

5. **Statistical language** - How to code statements using relative risk, odds ratios, or correlation coefficients (more correlational) vs. plain language claims (more causal)

These would have been resolved through:
- Consensus meetings among the coding team
- Reference to detailed examples in the coding manual
- Discussion rules (how to handle disagreement during double-coding)

## Notes for Your Codebook Development

1. **Scale depth matters**: The 7-point causal scale captures important distinctions (conditional vs. unconditional causality) that binary classification would miss
2. **Reliability testing is essential**: Their 91% concordance and κ=0.88 required careful operationalization—invest in defining boundary cases
3. **Transparent materials are replicable**: Your codebook should include explicit examples for each scale point
4. **Text location specificity**: Their choice to code titles and first sentences is strategic; consider which parts of a post carry most social media impact
5. **Supplementary materials are crucial**: Sumner's decision to publish all coding sheets enabled their methodology to be adopted by others (including replication studies published 2020)
